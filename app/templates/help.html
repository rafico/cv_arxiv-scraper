Help

Learn how to use arXiv CV Scraper to stay on top of computer vision research.

What is arXiv CV Scraper?

arXiv CV Scraper is a personal research paper aggregation tool for computer vision (cs.CV). It scrapes the daily arXiv RSS feed, matches papers against your interests — authors, institutions, and topics — and ranks them using a composite scoring system that learns from your feedback.

Everything runs locally on your machine. There is no signup, no cloud dependency, and no data leaves your computer unless you choose to send yourself email digests via Gmail.

Getting Started

  1. 1

    Add your interests

    Go to Settings → Research Setup and add authors, institutions/labs, and topic keywords you want to track.

  2. 2

    Run a scrape

    Click the Run Scrape button in the top-right corner. The scraper fetches the latest arXiv CS.CV feed, matches papers against your interests, and scores them. Progress is shown in a live toast notification.

  3. 3

    Triage your papers

    Browse matched papers on the Papers dashboard. Use Upvote, Save, or Skip to give feedback — the system learns from your actions and adjusts future rankings.

Using the Dashboard

Views

Switch between Inbox (all matched papers) and Saved (your shortlisted papers) using the tabs at the top of the page.

Filters & Search

Filter by timeframe (Daily, Weekly, Monthly, All Time), match type (Author, Affiliation, Title), or use free-text search to find papers across titles, authors, matched terms, and summaries.

Sorting

Trending sorts by combined score (match strength + feedback boost). Newest sorts by publication date.

Paper Cards

Each card shows the title, authors, publication date, match type badges (Author Affiliation Title), composite score, auto-generated summary, topic tags, and related papers.

Feedback Actions

  • Upvote — marks a paper as highly relevant and boosts similar future papers
  • Save — adds the paper to your permanent shortlist with the strongest boost
  • Skip — suppresses the paper and reduces ranking of similar content

Settings Overview

Research Setup

Add authors, institutions/labs, and topic keywords to track. Papers matching these signals will appear in your feed. One entry per line.

Ranking

Adjust the weight given to author, affiliation, and title matches. Configure the recency half-life to control how quickly older papers decay in score. Manage muted authors, topics, and affiliations to hide unwanted content.

Automation

Set up email digests by configuring a recipient address and connecting your Gmail account via OAuth. Once authorized, you can send yourself a digest of matched papers or automate it with a cron job.

LLM / AI

Optionally enable AI-generated summaries using OpenRouter or a local Ollama instance. When enabled, each matched paper receives a concise summary and an AI relevance score that feeds into the overall ranking.

How Scoring Works

Each paper receives a composite score that determines its ranking. The score is calculated from several components:

Score = (Match + Terms + Resources + AI) × Recency + Feedback
  • Match score — points for each type of match (Author, Affiliation, Title), weighted by your settings
  • Term score — bonus for each matched whitelist term (multiple matches stack)
  • Resource score — small bonus for papers with code or data links
  • AI bonus — LLM relevance score (only when LLM is enabled)
  • Recency multiplier — exponential decay based on paper age (configurable half-life)
  • Feedback bonus — accumulated from your upvotes, saves, and skips

You can view the full score breakdown for any paper by clicking on its score badge in the dashboard.

Tips

  • • Follow & Mute from paper cards — use the quick actions on any paper to follow its primary author or mute a topic without leaving the dashboard.
  • • Force re-scrape — if today's feed was already scraped, the system will offer a "Run Again" option to force a fresh fetch.
  • • Export reports — download an HTML report of your matched papers for any timeframe from the dashboard.
  • • Multiple matches rank higher — a paper by a tracked author from a tracked lab on a tracked topic will receive the highest composite score.
  • • Feedback improves over time — the more you upvote and save, the better the ranking adapts to your actual preferences.
